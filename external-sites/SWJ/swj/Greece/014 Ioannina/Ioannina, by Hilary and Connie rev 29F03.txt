<p class=MsoTitle align=left style='text-align:left'><b><span style='font-size:
28.0pt;font-family:"Monotype Corsiva";color:#993366'>Infidels in the Old City</span></b><b><span
style='font-size:28.0pt;font-family:"Monotype Corsiva";mso-bidi-font-family:
Arial;color:#993366'><o:p></o:p></span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:left;mso-layout-grid-align:none;
punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span
style='font-size:28.0pt;font-family:"Monotype Corsiva";mso-fareast-font-family:
PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>The oldest
synagogue in Ioannina existed in the 9<sup>th</sup> century C.E, and stood
until 1810. This may be evidence of the initial Jewish settlement here, or,
Jews may have arrived, as legend tells us, soon after the destruction of the 2<sup>nd</sup>
temple.</span><span style='font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:PMingLiU;
mso-bidi-font-family:Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;mso-layout-grid-align:
none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>After
various and many invasions by different nomadic tribes, the province of Epirus
and its capital Ioannina fell to Ottoman rule from 1430 until 1913. As non-Moslems,
Jews were second-class citizens; they paid a head tax to qualify for property
ownership and paid twice the customs duties as Moslems did at the borders. On
the other hand, they were free to worship and to organize their community with
considerable autonomy.</span><span style='font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:
PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>As
adherents to a monotheistic faith, their status was somewhat raised and they
were entitled to become diplomats, an occupation strict Muslims abhorred.
Travel throughout the Ottoman Empire allowed contact with other Jewish
communities and enabled some Ioannina Jews to become bankers and traders.
Others became tinsmiths, silver and goldsmiths; dyers, shoemakers and
manufacturers of veils, belts and embroidered garments.</span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:
Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>Sultan
Bayazid welcomed to his Ottoman lands the wealthy and educated Sephardi Jews,
expelled from Spain and Sicily in 1492. In Ioannina, these Sephardim thought
themselves superior to the indiginous Romaniot Jews and initially kept aloof.
Eventually, the two communities merged; Sephardi prayer books dominated,
although some Romaniot prayers and customs were retained, but Greek became the
common language.</span><span style='font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:
PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>With the
decline of the Ottoman Empire, the death of Suleiman the Great in 1566 marked a
sharp transition. Successor sultans were less tolerant; Jews were now forbidden
ostentatious manners in public---dressing in prohibited costume could result in
expulsion.</span><span style='font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:PMingLiU;
mso-bidi-font-family:Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;</span>A <i>kehillah</i> of 800 was recorded in
Ioannina in 1670, when 160 families engaged in silk manufacture. Rabbis
visiting from Palestine were warmly welcomed and often stayed for extended
periods. The rule of the cunning and cruel Ali Pasha from 1788 to 1822 brought
a period of prosperity for Ioannina and the <i>kehillah</i>. Ioannina developed
as a commercial center and<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>literary capital. Ali Pasha allowed the Jews religious freedom and
appointed Jews to his council.</span><span style='font-family:Verdana;
mso-fareast-font-family:PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:
ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>The <i>kehillah</i>
was originally contained in the <i>kastro, </i>the old walled city,<i> </i>with
its synagogue, the Kahal Kodesh Yashan. When the community grew it expanded
outside the city wall. This happened in 1540 when the <i>kehillah</i> numbered
5000 and again in 1841 when the community included more than 250 families. The
Kahal Kodesh Hadash synagogue of 1841 was built outside the wall on the
foundations of one from the 16<sup>th</sup> century.</span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:
Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>Christian &#8211;
Jewish friction, both religiously and politically based, escalated throughout
the 19<sup>th</sup> century causing 1,500 of the 4,000 Jews living in Ioannina
at the beginning of the 20<sup>th</sup> century to emigrate by 1906. Only the
poorest of the <i>kehillah</i> remained.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;
</span>Continuing economic difficulties and the lure of better opportunities
caused many younger Jews to continue emigrating from Ioannina and Greece so
that by World War II only some 2000 Jews remained. In March 1944, 1,870 Jews
were deported to Auschwitz. Only 164 returned.</span><span style='font-family:
Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:Tahoma;
mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>By the
1990s, 60 Jews still lived in a building in the Jewish quarter outside the old
city, on the site of the Kahal Kodesh Hadash (new) synagogue that was
demolished by the Germans. They continued to use the Kahal Kodesh Yashan (old)
synagogue in the <i>kastro,</i> however, since the mayor of Ionnanina,
Demetrios Vlachides, convinced the Germans to leave the building to the city as
a library. A plaque in his honor hangs in the preserved synagogue. </span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:
Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:11.0pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;mso-layout-grid-align:none;punctuation-wrap:simple;text-autospace:none;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>After the
war, the synagogue and hidden religious articles were returned to the
community. As a gesture of thanksgiving, the diminished <i>kehillah</i> sent
two of its Torah scrolls to Ioannina congregations abroad, one to Manhattan and
one to Jerusalem.<span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span></span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:PMingLiU;mso-bidi-font-family:
Tahoma;mso-fareast-language:ZH-TW'><o:p></o:p></span></p>
