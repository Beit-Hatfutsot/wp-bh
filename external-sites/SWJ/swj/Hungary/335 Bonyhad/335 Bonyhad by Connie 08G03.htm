A Blue Stucco Baldachin

 

        When the Turks were defeated in the 18th century, Jews were among those who came from Austria, Germany and Moravia to repopulate the Hungarian plain between the Danube River and the Austrian border.  Resident estate owners gave them permits to settle in the town of Bonyhad, about 93 miles south of Budapest. 

       

Here the Jews engaged in trade and established factories, the most famous one being a factory for smoking pipes that were sold to gentlemen in Western Europe.  The Jews boasted a popular chief of police, maintained good relations with their neighbors and demonstrated loyalty to Hungary by fighting for it in1848-9 alongside Christian soldiers.

 

By the mid 19th century, the town’s 1500 Jews constituted a third of its total population.  In 1869, the split between the liberal Neolog majority and the conservative Orthodox minority in Hungarian Jewry echoed in Bonyhad.  The Orthodox broke away and set up their own community – from schools and charities to a matzah factory and a cemetery. They eventually built their own synagogue in1924, on the corner of Zsido Koz (Jews’ Alley). The older synagogue, erected by community volunteers in 1795, remained with the Neolog faction. It had replaced the original wooden synagogue that was destroyed by a fire in 1794. All the Jewish community buildings and many homes were burned as well.

       

Bonyhad maintained a strong Jewish population, numbering more than 1200 until the deportations in 1944. Only 170 survivors returned.

 

The communists transformed both the Orthodox and the Neolog synagogues into storerooms. Ignac Warum, an Orthodox community leader, and other returnees from WW II labor camps buried the damaged Torah scrolls found on the Orthodox synagogue floor. No other hint of Jewishness remained in the building, not even a Hebrew inscription.

 

The Neolog synagogue faired better. The Zopf style building façade has prominent pilasters under a hip roof and an elaborate double portal of stone on the south side. Inside, although chipped in many places, the stucco Aron Kodesh towers 23 feet high, flanked by Ionic pillars and pilasters. Over the ark, reminiscent of opulent draperies is a blue stucco baldachin with gold fringe, drawstrings and tassels. Similar decorative elements appear in synagogues in nearby Baja and Apostag. This ornate Baroque style is typical of the work of itinerant court artisans who were occasionally permitted by local noblemen to embellish the synagogues of their “Schutz-Juden”, protected Jews. 

 

Built in the “Lemberg” (Polish) style, four pillars divide the ceiling into nine bays and mark the area where the central square bimah once stood.  Two slender wooden posts support the U-shaped women’s gallery above.  Faintly visible Baroque floral designs adorn the deeply vaulted ceiling above the gallery that is bounded by a wooden balustrade and a decorative metal grille.  An elegant gallery staircase in a two-story west annex was added in 1896 when the community had grown and prospered.

 

Mrs. Ignac Warum lives in a little cottage on Zsido Koz near the two synagogues and she still maintains her husband’s glass shop, her piety and her kosher diet. She is the only Jewish widow who didn’t remarry a gentile after WW II and therefore considers herself the only real Jew left in this town.

 

Another Bonyhad resident, Jung Heydrich, member of the Shwabs - a German minority, remembered his father working with the Jews before the Nazis and the Russians changed everything. As owners of the water mill, they ground the wheat grown by the Jews into flour that was sold in town and they even cleaned the mill thoroughly before Passover so that the flour could be used for baking Matzot. His father considered the Jews his friends and Heydrich hoped there were still Jews alive in Israel who remembered the old mill.
