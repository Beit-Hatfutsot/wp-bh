Yeshivas in the Wine Country

 

            M d, also known as Hegyalja-M d, is located in northeast Hungary, just 23 kilometers from Miskolc. Famous for its wine production, the area has attracted Jews since the time of the Middle-Ages, when they would come for the grape-harvesting season. By the early-eighteenth century there were a few who had received permission to settle in M d, and most of them became involved in the wine business, either through production or sales. Their Hegyalja wine soon earned a good reputation, and was known throughout Hungary.

 

        This success in the wine industry also earned the Jews enemies among their non-Jewish neighbors. In 1812, with 76 Jewish families already living in M d, the local Christians sent an official complaint against them. The committee that investigated the matter recommended that all Jews who settled in M d after 1790 be banished. This committee also recommended that the complainants file a suit against Jews practicing usury and that they should compel Jewish owners of vineyard to sell their property to Christians. Most of these recommendations were not carried out, yet the friction between the two communities remained. Again in 1828 there was a request to the town’s authorities to banish all Jews from M d.

 

        Despite this anti-Semitism, the Jewish community in M d flourished. The city had one of the most important Orthodox communities in Hungary, and was known even outside the country for its yeshiva. This fame was due to the many respected scholar-rabbis who made M d their home. Among them were Rabbi Natan Feitel Reinitz, author of Divrei Ranaf; Rabbi Amnon Blum, author of Responsa and Beit Sh’arim; and Rabbi Mordecai Winkler, whose Responsa was later published as Levushay Mordecai. The yeshiva played such an important part in the lives of the Jews that they closed the government school in favor of the heder. They opened it again in 1813 only after coercion from the local authorities. This—as well as the 200 yeshiva students and the 50 Torah scrolls in M d before World War II—testify to the level of religious learning that once existed there.

 

        The testy relationship between Jews and Christians deteriorated further in 1938, when the Discrimination Laws were published. In 1941 10-15 families who could not prove Hungarian citizenship were transferred to Kamenecz-Podolsk, where they died. The year after that, Jewish youth were seized for forced labor and sent to the Ukraine; only ten of the young people survived. Finally, after the German occupation began in 1944, the remaining Jews of M d were transferred to the district capital, Satoraljaujhely, and from there to Auschwitz. Of the few Jews who survived the Holocaust, only 28 returned to M d. Today there are no Jews left in the town.

 

Their synagogue, however, was declared a historic monument in 1946 and remains there to this day. Built in 1798, it is a beautiful, Baroque-style shul. By legend, each stone used in its construction was individually carried by a member of the community, so that no strange hands would touch the material of the sacred building. Now that that community is gone, the only ones left to care for the shul are gentiles. It is currently being restored under the supervision of the Herman Otto Museum of Miskolc. There may not be many people left to attest to the authenticity of this restoration, but each year a group of  eleven Holocaust survivors, born in M d, reassemble there from the far corners of the world.
