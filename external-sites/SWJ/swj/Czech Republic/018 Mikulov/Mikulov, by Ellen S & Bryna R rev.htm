Half on Foot, Half on Horseback

 

Mikulov, known in German as Nikolsburg, is a strategic castle promontory in the Pavlov hills just southeast of Brno. It began to attract Jews in the 15th century after they were expelled from Austria (1438) and the Moravian royal cities (1454). The Jews were drawn to Mikulov both for its location on the trade route between Vienna and Brno, and for the protection they found under its sovereign and his castle's walls. They could buy and own property in Mikulov and they faced little threat of expulsion. They were, however, often subject to the whims of the aristocracy. As an instance of this, in 1560, the Count decreed that Jews must give 40 days of annual service to the lords, “half on foot and half on horseback,” ---labor with and without harness and plow.

Despite the unfair treatment they received, the Jews enjoyed enough stability to form a tightly organized kehillah, which prospered in the local wine industry. They were able and willing to help other Jews in distress, and when survivors of the Chmielnicki Massacres in Poland (1648-49) made their way to Mikulov, they received a warm welcome. These Polish Jews, in turn, brought a Hasidic influence to the religious life of the community. Eventually the generosity of Mikulov’s Jews would be rewarded. When, in 1719, a fire destroyed the Jewish quarter, and the community was forced to rebuild their homes in brick, Jews from all over Europe sent donations to help with the cost of reconstructing the quarter.

Mikulov was the primary seat of the Moravian chief rabbinate from the 16th to the19th centuries. Many prominent rabbis and scholars were drawn to its famous yeshivot. The legendary Rabbi Yehudah Loew ben Bezalel officiated there (r. 1553-1573) until he left for Prague. Hasidism was encouraged in the community by the kabbalist Rabbi Shmuel Ben Hirsch Horowitz (r. 1773-1778), and then again by Talmudist Rabbi Mordechai ben Abraham Benet (r. 1789-1829). The last chief rabbi from Mikulov was Rabbi Samson Rafael Hirsch (r. 1847-1851), father of a neo-Orthodox educational system that became popular in Germany and elsewhere. Since the mid 16th century, rabbis who passed away in Mikulov were honored by burial in the Rabbis’ Mount area of the cemetery, which even today remains a site of pilgrimage for their followers. The cemetery itself is one of the most important in Moravia. Its 4,000 gravestones are archetypes of Moravian Jewish funerary art, displaying both the plant motifs of Moravian culture along with Jewish symbols: crowns, lions and inverted scrolls.

At first, the Jews of Mikulov were involved mainly in the retail or wine trades, but by the 17th century, vocations shifted towards crafts and professions. Furriers, tailors, goldsmiths, physicians and musicians were represented in the kehillah. Larger groups of tradesmen formed guilds and prayed together at their own small synagogues. By the early 19th century, there were 12 synagogues in the city. The Jews comprised 42 percent of the town’s population and maintained ritual baths, hospital, yeshivah, and school for girls.

When the Vienna-Brno railroad was built in 1841, the route bypassed Mikulov, which ceased to be a major junction. As a result, many families left the overcrowded ghetto for the larger terminal cities, where they were newly permitted to settle. As time went on, the Jewish population dwindled and with it the number of synagogues. By 1919, only the upper Altschul and the lower Neuschul were functioning. For the Jews who stayed behind, however, opportunities opened up and the economic development of the town owed a lot to them. Jewish enterprises included a clothing mill, leather factory and a grain distribution center, among others.

During World War I, thousands of destitute Jews from Galicia found safety in Mikulov, but the post-war economic decline drove most of them away. Whatever Jews were left in the city became jeopardized by the Munich Pact of 1938 that ceded Mikulov to the Germans as part of the Sudentenland. Whoever could, fled the city for the countryside, enduring years of fear and deprivation. In 1941, deportation squads found 39 Mikulov Jews in hiding and promptly dispatched them to Theresienstadt. The few Jews who returned after the war met open hostility from the gentiles who were occupying their houses, and left. Today, there are no Jews in Mikulov.

In the 1970’s, the Neuschul synagogue was torn down by the Communist regime after years of neglect. Alarmed, historians and researchers of the Regional Museum of Mikulov mobilized to rescue and restore the Altschul synagogue. Expert craftsmen worked on the synagogue painstakingly but intermittently for 12 years, as a lengthy struggle for funds took place. Now renovated, the building serves as the town’s cultural center. Inside, where the Ark once stood, are glass-enclosed cases for displaying Mikulov’s Judaica, due to return from the Jewish museum in Prague. The festive reopening of the synagogue took place on September 23, 1992, with a concert of classical and Israeli selections performed by Jerusalem musicians. Town residents and dignitaries joined guests from near and far and, after a half-century hiatus, Hebrew music was heard again within those walls.
