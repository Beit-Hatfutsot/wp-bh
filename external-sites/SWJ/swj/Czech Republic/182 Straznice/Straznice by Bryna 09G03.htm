Of Old, But Uncertain Origin

 

Close to the Slovakian border, the pleasant Moravian town of Straznice lies in a valley south of the Morava river. Its entrance is still guarded by the remnants of two massive tower-topped stone gates from the 16th century. The nearby castle contains a museum of Moravian folk art and the town hosts a folk festival every year.

 

The earliest documents of Jewish settlement date back to 1490, but it is believed that Jews arrived in the area as much as a century earlier. By 1669 there were 55 Jewish families living in 44 houses. In 1780 there were 84 Jews; in 1848, 568 Jews lived in 80 houses, and in 1900 there were 323,diminished to l94 by 1930. An attempt to revive the small Jewish community after WWII failed as the few survivors drifted away to the capitals or emigrated.

 

Two important rabbis who officiated in Straznice achieved fame elsewhere later on: Rabbi Shabbtai Ben Meir Hacohen, at Straznice from 1650 to 1657, became known as Rav Shach of Holesov; Rabbi Moses Schreiber, originally from Frankfort, served in Straznice from 1794 to 1798 and later gained renown as the Hatam Sofer of Bratislava. His subterranean grave there is preserved under modern road works and is still a shrine for his followers.

 

The original Jewish quarter of Straznice suffered much damage during the Hussite wars of the early 15th century, generally a period of tribulation for the Jews. By the late 15th and 16th centuries an alternate locale was developed for Jewish residence at the opposite end of town. This ghetto lies behind today's sloping, cobble-stone Bzenecka street, recently spruced up with colorful flower beds along the house fronts. One can enter through either of two narrow alleys, one between the first two houses on the street, the other between houses Number 35 and Number 37. Short paths lead to the small unpaved square, now Sadova street, where there are relics of yesterday's world: the synagogue, the long, low ceremonial hall, and behind these two buildings, the large old cemetery exceptionally located adjacent to the synagogue. Nearby, stands a cluster of former Jewish houses in varied states of restoration. Fresh white stucco walls contrasted sharply with mounds of old bricks from demolished buildings heaped up under a tree in the little square.

 

The synagogue is of old, but uncertain origin. It was rebuilt in neo-Moresque style after 1896, and modified in 1906. After the Shoah, the building was put to use as a furniture storeroom, and floor space was increased by the addition of a floor halfway up the prayer room. The top portion of a mutilated stucco Aron is still visible at this upper level, whereas the truncated lower halves of the Aron pillars are barely visible through the debris piled up on the floor below. A few fragments of Hebrew inscriptions on the barrel vaulted ceiling remain; the ceiling is painted blue and is lavishly bedecked with large white flowers.

 

Although the cemetery is bounded by a crumbling brick wall, the entrance to it is through a noticeably recent wooden gate at the southwest corner. Just inside the gate and high on the western facade of the synagogue, a row of large holes in the bricks extends above a series of deep, arched alcoves. On ground level, this area was once the location of the extension that served as the women's prayer room, demolished during WWII. The alcoves were formerly open, for contact with the synagogue interior in the manner of other ancient synagogues such as Sopron in Hungary and the Altneuschul in Prague. This rare arrangement suggests that the synagogue was first built perhaps as early as the 17th century. Entrance to the cemetery had originally been through the adjoining ceremonial hall. With the demolition of the extension, it became possible to provide an entry directly from the courtyard.

 

The cemetery is tended by a local woman, Ilona Barankova, whose Jewish mother is buried there. Ilona opened the gate for us, and came with her grandson to lay flowers on her mother's grave. It was marked by a large gray marble stone, with a faded, hardly legible Hebrew inscription above and a new black marble band with Czech inscription added recently, below.
