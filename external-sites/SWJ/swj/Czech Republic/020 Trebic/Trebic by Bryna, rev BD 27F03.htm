Angels to Guard You in All Your Ways

 

Trěbíč is a highland town near Brno that overlooks the Jihlava Valley. Although the earliest date of Jewish settlement in Trěbíč is unclear, its existence in the region is documented from 1286, when a local statute separated the Jews from Christians. They were concentrated into a quarter on the banks of the Jihlava River called Zámostí (behind the bridge). Like most medieval ghettos in Europe, it was close to the castle of the local lord, far from the sight of his visitors, but not beyond the range of his greed. Trěbíč Jews often had to pay for living in peace, as in 1556, when a protection tax was imposed on them to pay for half the expenses of the manorial guards. They also had to pay 120 gold coins annually for the privilege of not lending horses to the manor.

In spite of the taxes levied on them and their cramped conditions in Zámostí, the Jews prospered. By the 16th century they operated most of the local branches of commerce and were so successful that their Christian competitors complained. In response, the Count of Boskovice would have expelled them from Trěbíč, but he was deterred from such action by his dependence on the Jewish contribution to the defense of Moravia. However, the Jews of Zámostí suffered cruelty and pillage by Hungarian troops on their way to fight the Turks.

Besides commerce, the Jews in the community worked in lumber processing, soap manufacture, distilling and tanning. They were also active in the production and export of a fine cloth—trebitscher Tuche—famous for its beauty. In 1642, the kehillah built a substantial synagogue to replace an older wooden one. Originally called the Front Synagogue because of its proximity to Zámostí’s entrance, it eventually became known as the Altschul (old synagoge) when a newer synagogue, the Neuschul, was built, also in the 17th century. The Altschul was built higher than the neighboring houses, but because the sight of the Sabbath candles burning brightly in its windows irritated the Countess of the castle, the Jews were forced to lower the building in 1757.

The Jews, however, did not always back down in the face of their oppressors. When Count Valdstein took over Trěbíč at the turn of the 18th century, he found a kehillah that was large and powerful, and when he tried to enact anti-Jewish measures that disregarded their rights, the Jews initiated court proceedings against him. Their case was taken all the way to the court of Kaiser Joseph I, who sided with the Jewish community. Another, less successful episode can be found in 1821, when the community was ordered to refrain from celebrating Purim because it fell on the same day as Holy Friday. The soldiers who came to enforce the law were chased away by the Jews, but later returned to beat many of them with sticks. Still, the chutzpah that they showed proves that the community possessed strength, and their numbers in Trěbíč back that up. By the end of the 18th century there were 1,770 Jews in the town---59% of the total population.

These figures began to decline in 1861, when the Emancipation opened up bigger cities, like Vienna, to Jewish settlement. Even though the Jews enjoyed some power in Trěbíč, their circumstances were still harsh. Every year they could expect the river to flood their homes, and the overcrowding in the ghetto brought unsanitary conditions and illness. By the end of World War I the kehillah had lost 20 men and was down to 344 members. They assembled only in the Altschul after this, and the Neuschul was used for storage.

Jews left in Trěbíč. They, along with 1,370 Jews from the southwest of Moravia, were deported to concentration camps in 1942. A few women returned after the war, but there was no interest in renovating the synagogues and they fell into disrepair. The Altschul was eventually bought by the Czech Brethren in 1952, and used as a church. At the time it was essentially stripped of all its Jewish associations when they renovated it to suit their needs. Not much notice was given to the Neuschul until a proposal to demolish the entire ghetto emerged in the 70’s. It was too costly to rebuild the area from scratch, so it was decided that they would renovate instead. Now Zámostí is a protected, historic zone, and the Neuschul has been refurbished to serve as a cultural hall and museum.

Since 1996, when Marta Friedová died, there have been no Jews in Trěbíč, but they do have one representative: a lively octogenarian named Bohumír Pavlik. A former teacher, he has worked in the cemetery since his retirement, maintaining the grounds. During Communist rule he hid a few Jewish objects that he restored to the cemetery’s ceremonial hall. One of the objects, a black velvet burial cloth used to cover the bier, carries an inscription which could be said to describe Pavlik’s work in Trěbíč: “For he will order his angels to guard you wherever you go” (Ps. 91:11).
