<p class=MsoPlainText style='text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;direction:ltr;

mso-fareast-font-family:"MS Mincho";mso-fareast-language:EN-US'>The Jewish

ghetto, established under an edict of</span><span dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL
style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:

mso-fareast-font-family:"MS Mincho";mso-fareast-language:EN-US'>Emperor Charles
VI. It lies a short distance from the circularly shaped main square of this
small Bohemian town. Until WWII not more than a single narrow lane connected
the Jewish enclave with the rest of the town. The first Jew, Jakub Mali,
settled here about 1570, and by 1618 four Jewish families were listed in the
town roster. It was always a small community, however, numbering some 160-170
persons at the maximum, between 1774 and 1786. No more than 20-30 Jewish
families lived in Kasejovice during</span><span dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL
style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-fareast-font-family:

mso-fareast-font-family:"MS Mincho";mso-fareast-language:EN-US'>the 18th and
19th centuries, a period of flourishing growth for most Bohemian Jewish
communities. <o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoPlainText style='text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;direction:ltr;
unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana;
mso-fareast-font-family:"MS Mincho";mso-fareast-language:EN-US'>The extension
of civil rights to Jews in 1848 stimulated many of them to leave Kasejovice for
the larger cities that offered more opportunities, or for America. One hundred
fifteen Jews still resided in Kasejovice in 1904, but the number decreased
steadily thereafter, along with the general depopulation of the southern
Bohemian region, and by 1930 only 28 remained.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoPlainText style='text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;direction:ltr;
unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana;
mso-fareast-font-family:"MS Mincho";mso-fareast-language:EN-US'>While still
growing in 1763, the community built its synagogue in a small open space within
a ring of 14 simple houses. In Rococo style, the synagogue is quite ornate for
so small a community. The building is noteworthy for its 'Kasula' form windows,
such as are often found in Catholic churches of that period. A short flight of
stairs up from the main entrance leads directly into the prayer room. There is
no sign of a separate women's gallery. Presumably, the women sat at the back of
the room. <o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoPlainText style='text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;direction:ltr;

mso-fareast-font-family:"MS Mincho";mso-fareast-language:EN-US'>The Rabbi's
apartment consisted of three small rooms, below the sanctuary. The synagogue
exterior underwent restoration in the summer of 1992 and the interior was

of wood on a dark gray base, dating from the reconstruction of 1830. The four
panels of its doors are embellished with gold Stars of David. The Aron is
outlined by two pairs of pillars and a graceful, Baroque frame resting on Ionic
capitals, all painted to simulate light gray marble. Above this structure and
within a draped triangular cartouche topped by a gilded crown, a gilded inscription
declares: &quot;Moses entrusted us with the Torah-- a tradition for the
congregation of Jacob</span><span dir=RTL></span><span lang=HE dir=RTL

mso-fareast-font-family:"MS Mincho";mso-fareast-language:EN-US'>Holocaust

wooden Christian icons, probably from the 18th century. We remarked to our
hosts that in that location the icons constituted a religious incongruity. Our
hosts were sensitive to the hint. The icons were quickly moved elsewhere and we
were assured that the separation would permanently be observed.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
