Icons on the Bimah

 

The Jewish quarter of Kasejovice, locally known as Zidovske mesto, is a former ghetto, established under an edict of Emperor Charles VI. It lies a short distance from the circularly shaped main square of this small Bohemian town. Until WWII not more than a single narrow lane connected the Jewish enclave with the rest of the town. The first Jew, Jakub Mali, settled here about 1570, and by 1618, four Jewish families were listed in the town roster. It was always a small community, however, numbering some 160-170 persons at the maximum, between 1774 and 1786. No more than 20-30 Jewish families lived in Kasejovice during the 18th and 19th centuries, a period of flourishing growth for most Bohemian Jewish communities.

The extension of civil rights to Jews in 1848 stimulated many of them to leave Kasejovice for the larger cities that offered more opportunities, or for America. One hundred fifteen Jews still resided in Kasejovice in 1904, but the number decreased steadily thereafter, along with the general depopulation of the southern Bohemian region, and by 1930 only 28 remained.

On Nov. 26, l942, 42 Jews from Kasejovice and the neighboring town of Breznice were transported to Terezin. One woman, alone, returned to Kasejovice. The names of thirty-six Jewish victims of the Nazis from Kasejovice are engraved on a black marble memorial tablet that was erected in a little park area on the edge of the former ghetto.

While still growing in 1763, the community built its synagogue in a small open space within a ring of 14 simple houses. In Rococo style, the synagogue is quite ornate for so small a community. The building is noteworthy for its 'Kasula' form windows, such as are often found in Catholic churches of that period. A short flight of stairs up from the main entrance leads directly into the prayer room. There is no sign of a separate women's gallery. Presumably, the women sat at the back of the room.

The Rabbi's apartment consisted of three small rooms, below the sanctuary. The synagogue exterior underwent restoration in the summer of 1992 and the interior was renovated later. Still present and in good condition is a Baroque Aron Kodesh of wood on a dark gray base, dating from the reconstruction of 1830. The four panels of its doors are embellished with gold Stars of David. The Aron is outlined by two pairs of pillars and a graceful, Baroque frame resting on Ionic capitals, all painted to simulate light gray marble. Above this structure and within a draped triangular cartouche topped by a gilded crown, a gilded inscription declares: "Moses entrusted us with the Torah-- a tradition for the congregation of Jacob." (Deut. 33:4). A chronogram designates the year as 5590 (1830).

Holocaust survivor, Vaclav Mentberger (1886-~1970), an avid collector of Bohemian folk art and Judaica, returned to Kasejovice when he retired from the state railways system. He prevailed upon the local authorities to designate the vacant synagogue as the town museum. He restored the building and transferred his private collection there, bequeathing it to the town. He also organized the remaining records of Kasejovice's Jewish community.

When we entered the synagogue, we saw on the platform before the Aron, a display of carved-wood Christian icons, probably from the 18th century. We remarked to our hosts that in that location the icons constituted a religious incongruity. Our hosts were sensitive to the hint. The icons were quickly moved elsewhere and we were assured that the separation would permanently be observed.

In the museum storeroom, in another building, a few torn and decaying Torah scrolls lay scattered on a low table. Ironically, they might have been saved if the Nazis had sent them to Prague. Many scrolls from synagogues throughout Czechoslovakia were collected in Prague. Most of them were repaired and distributed after the war to Jewish congregations in many parts of the world.

The cemetery in Kasejovice, established in 1669, overlooks the town from a hilltop to the north. Many interesting Baroque and classical tombstones from the 17th and 18th centuries remain. Within the crumbling walls of the roofless ceremonial hall there used to stand the decrepit remains of a horse-drawn funeral wagon.
