A Jewish History

 

Formerly Czechoslovakia, from 1918 until World War II and again after the war, until Slovakia seceded in 1993, the Czech Republic lies in the heart of Europe. The national capital, Prague, is also the capital of Bohemia the western province, whereas the province of Moravia in the southeast has a capital in Brno.

The Jewish presence in the Czech lands dates from the beginning of the 10th century, C.E., when Jews immigrated from Germany, Hungary and the Byzantine Empire. Most came as traders or workers in the crafts and were permitted to set up autonomous communities near the markets and trade routes. The main Jewish community developed eventually near Prague's old town, on the right bank of the Vltava River.

Jews and Christians had a tumultuous relationship that alternated under anti-Semitic laws between bouts of tolerance and persecution. The period of the Crusades was fraught with pogrom and massacre of Jews, but a symbiotic association developed between the nobility and the Jews. In return for high taxes and generous loans, Jews gained permission to settle and some protection from abuse. There were occasional expulsions from Czech cities, but these were normally followed by reprieves a few years later.

In the 16th and 17th centuries, there was enough stability to allow the Jews an interval of economic and cultural prosperity. It was during this era that some of the most famous synagogues of Prague were built, and the city itself became a center for Jewish life. By the end of the 17th century, the Jewish community in Prague was the third largest in the world, numbering 11,500 members.

In 1726 Czech Jews again faced discrimination, when the Familiants Law was enacted, permitting only one son of each Jewish family to marry. This, along with a revival of ghettoization, caused large-scale emigration, mostly to Poland or to Slovakia, then a province of Hungary. It was not until 1782 when Joseph II promulgated the Edict of Toleration that things began to look up for the Jewish community. Economic and residence restrictions were abolished, and secular university education was opened up to them. Although the Familiants Law remained in force, the edict granted the Jews official recognition and religious freedom.

This new freedom came with a price: Jewish judicial autonomy was revoked, adoption of the German language for family names and for business use (instead of Hebrew) became mandatory and military conscription was applied. Overall, however, the Jewish population benefited from the new status and entered a period of rapid population growth, peaking in 1890 with 139,923 Czech Jews. Numbers dwindled from internal rather than external factors after the turn of the 20th century, assimilation, low birth rate and migration.

The Munich Pact of September 30, 1938, ceded the Sudetenland to Germany, and six months later the Nazi troops occupying Bohemia and Moravia began the systematic destruction of Czech Jewry. Of the 122,000 persons in the prewar community, 89,000 were deported. The names of the 78,000 Czech Jews who perished were inscribed on the walls of the Pinkus Synagogue in Prague. In 1945, when about 10,000 deportees returned to the Czech lands, they found themselves in a new struggle to maintain their Jewish culture. This time, the Communist regime prevented them from practicing their religion. Rather than fight it, many Jews emigrated to Israel and the West. At the end of the 20th century there were only 6,000 Jews left in the Czech Republic, most of them elderly.

The Velvet Revolution in 1989, which ended Communist rule in Czechoslovakia, led to a renewed interest in Jewish heritage on the part of individuals as well as civic and national authorities. Despite the destruction of synagogues during the war, and further demolition during the Communist period, more than 200 synagogues have survived in Bohemia and Moravia. Czech authorities now recognize the historical and cultural importance of these Jewish sites as part of their own heritage and as tourist attractions. A number of Czech municipalities have initiated programs of synagogue restoration for cultural purposes. Some have achieved excellent artistic results and a high level of historical authenticity. The existence of remarkably well-preserved ghettos has stimulated those towns to declare the ghettos National Heritage Zones. The restored Jewish Town in Prague, centered around the Altneuschul and the other old synagogues, is already one of the most popular tourist sites in Europe.
