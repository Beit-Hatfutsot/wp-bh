<h1 dir=LTR><span style='font-size:28.0pt;color:#993366'>The Czech Republic<o:p></o:p></span></h1>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:28.0pt'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span
style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Formerly
Czechoslovakia, from 1918 until World War II and again after the war, until
Slovakia seceded in 1993, the Czech Republic lies in the heart of Europe. The
national capital, Prague, is also the capital of Bohemia the western province,
whereas the province of Moravia in the southeast has a capital in Brno.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>The Jewish presence in the Czech lands dates from the beginning of the
10<sup>th</sup> century, C.E., when Jews immigrated from Germany, Hungary, and
the Byzantine Empire. Most came as</span><span dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL
style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:
Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span> </span><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>traders or workers in the crafts and were permitted to set up
autonomous communities near the markets and trade routes. The main Jewish<span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp; </span>community developed eventually near
Prague's old town, on the right bank of the Vltava River</span><span dir=RTL></span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;
mso-hansi-font-family:Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span>.</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span dir=LTR></span><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;</span><span style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Jews
and Christians had a tumultuous relationship that alternated under anti-Semitic
laws between bouts of tolerance and persecution. The period of the Crusades was
fraught with pogrom and massacre of Jews, but a symbiotic association developed
between the nobility and the Jews. In return for high taxes and generous loans,
Jews gained permission to settle and some protection from abuse. There were
occasional expulsions from Czech cities, but these were normally followed by
reprieves a few years later</span><span dir=RTL></span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:
Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span>.</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span dir=LTR></span><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>In the 16<sup>th</sup> and 17<sup>th</sup> centuries there was enough
stability to allow the Jews an interval of economic and cultural prosperity. It
was during this era that some of the most famous synagogues of Prague were
built, and the city itself became a center for Jewish life.</span><span
dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:
Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span> </span><span
style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'>By the end of the 17<sup>th</sup>
century, the Jewish community in Prague was the third largest in the world,
numbering 11,500 members</span><span dir=RTL></span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:
Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span>.</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span dir=LTR></span><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;</span>In 1726 Czech Jews again faced
discrimination, when the Familiants Law was enacted, permitting only one son of
each Jewish family to marry. This, along with a revival of ghettoization,
caused large-scale emigration, mostly to Poland or</span><span dir=RTL></span><span
dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:
Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span> </span><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>to Slovakia, then a province of Hungary. It was not until 1782 when
Joseph II promulgated the Edict of Toleration, that things began to look up for
the Jewish community. Economic and residence restrictions were abolished, and
secular university education was opened up to them. Although the Familiants Law
remained in force, the edict granted the Jews official recognition and
religious freedom.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>This new freedom came with a price: Jewish judicial autonomy was
revoked, adoption of the German language for</span><span dir=RTL></span><span
dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:
Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span> </span><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>family names and for business use (instead of Hebrew) became mandatory
and military conscription was applied. Overall, however, the Jewish population
benefited from the new status and entered a period of rapid population growth,
peaking in 1890 with</span><span dir=RTL></span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:
Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span> 139,923 </span><span style='font-size:11.0pt;
font-family:Verdana'>Czech Jews. Numbers dwindled from internal rather than
external factors after the turn of the 20th century,<span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">&nbsp; </span>assimilation, low birth rate, and migration. <o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>The Munich</span><span dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL style='font-size:
11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:Verdana'><span
dir=RTL></span> </span><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'>Pact
of September 30, 1938, ceded the Sudetenland to Germany, and six months later
the Nazi troops</span><span dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;
mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span>
</span><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'>occupying Bohemia and
Moravia began the systematic destruction of Czech Jewry. Of the 122,000 persons
in the prewar community, 89,000 were deported. The names of the 78,000 Czech
Jews who perished were inscribed on the walls of the Pinkus Synagogue in
Prague. In 1945, when about 10,000 deportees returned to the Czech lands, they
found themselves in a new struggle to maintain their Jewish culture. This time,
it was the Communist regime that prevented them from practicing their religion.
Rather than fight it, many Jews</span><span dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL
style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:
Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span> </span><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>emigrated to Israel and the West</span><span dir=RTL></span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;
mso-hansi-font-family:Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span>.</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span dir=LTR></span> At the end
of the 20<sup>th</sup> century there were only 6,000 Jews left in the Czech
Republic, most of them elderly.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span
style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;</span>The Velvet Revolution in 1989, which
ended Communist rule in Czechoslovakia, led to a renewed interest in Jewish
heritage on the part of individuals as well as civic and national authorities.</span><span
dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:
Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span> </span><span
style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'>Despite the destruction of
synagogues during the war, and further demolition during the Communist period,
more than 200 synagogues have survived</span><span dir=RTL></span><span
dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;mso-hansi-font-family:
Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span> </span><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:
Verdana'>in Bohemia and Moravia. Czech authorities now recognize the historical
and cultural importance of these Jewish sites as part of their own heritage and
as tourist attractions. A number of Czech municipalities have initiated
programs of synagogue restoration for cultural purposes. Some have achieved
excellent artistic results and a high level of historical authenticity. The
existence of remarkably well-preserved ghettos has stimulated those towns to
declare the ghettos National Heritage Zones. The restored Jewish Town in
Prague, centered around the Altneuschul and the other old synagogues, is
already one of the most popular tourist sites in Europe</span><span dir=RTL></span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:11.0pt;mso-ascii-font-family:Verdana;
mso-hansi-font-family:Verdana'><span dir=RTL></span>.</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><span dir=LTR></span><o:p></o:p></span></p>
