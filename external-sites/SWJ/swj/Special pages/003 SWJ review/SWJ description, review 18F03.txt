<h1><b><span style='font-size:14.0pt'>Synagogues Without Jews<o:p></o:p></span></b></h1>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-63.35pt;text-align:left;direction:ltr;
unicode-bidi:embed'><b><i><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana;
color:#993366'><span style="mso-spacerun: yes">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span>---and the communities that built and used them<o:p></o:p></span></i></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-3.35pt;text-align:left;direction:ltr;
unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-size:11.0pt;font-family:Verdana'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-3.35pt;text-align:left;text-indent:
36.0pt;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>&quot;The
isolated ruins lay hushed in the late afternoon sun as we clambered the low
walls and paced the broken pavement. A fallen architrave, engraved with ancient
Jewish symbols of a menorah, shofar, <i>lulav</i>, and <i>etrog</i>, lay on the
ground near the remains of a high semi-circular brick structure, thought to be
the Holy Ark niche. We photographed the site as a matter of course. We did not
know then that we had stumbled on a mission&quot;. <o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:11.25pt;margin-right:-3.35pt;margin-bottom:
0cm;margin-left:0cm;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>&quot;By
the time we arrived in Piedmont and canvassed its synagogues, enclaves of art
and tradition, we realized that we were on a new course. More of Renaissance
Italy could wait. These synagogue treasures -- the legacy of scattered Jewish
communities that dotted the hills -- must be documented.&quot; <o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:11.25pt;margin-right:-3.35pt;margin-bottom:
0cm;margin-left:0cm;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>After five
years of fieldwork and seven additional years of research the project that
began as an inspiration evolved into a coffee table size book illustrated with
over 300 color photographs of synagogues in 30 small Jewish communities located
in central and southern Europe. The art and architecture of these synagogues
underscore the centrality of the synagogue to the life of a Jewish community
and the book is premised on the idea that synagogues are the &quot;most significant
clue to the public aesthetics of Jewish life.&quot; <o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:11.25pt;margin-right:-3.35pt;margin-bottom:
0cm;margin-left:0cm;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><i><span style='font-family:Verdana'>Synagogues
Without Jews</span></i><span style='font-family:Verdana'> focuses on
lesser-known synagogues that are often the &quot;sole reminders of an
enterprising Jewish existence that once extended to every hamlet.&quot; There
was a real sense of urgency in doing this work as the authors explain: &quot;As
we researched we were literally racing against time, against indifference,
deterioration, and demolition.&quot; <o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:11.25pt;margin-right:-3.35pt;margin-bottom:
0cm;margin-left:0cm;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>In one town
the synagogue is now used as a barn, in another it had been torn down 11 months
earlier, and in others they are storerooms, workshops, apartments, churches. In
addition to describing the spiritual, artistic, and economic achievements of
Jews from these communities, the authors met and interviewed Jewish and
non-Jewish residents who remember when these were thriving Jewish communities.
The interviews were transcribed and translated by volunteers in Israel and
these memories are another significant contribution of the book. <o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:11.25pt;margin-right:-3.35pt;margin-bottom:
0cm;margin-left:0cm;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:left;text-indent:36.0pt;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>When they
embarked on this work of love, the authors had no idea that so prestigious a
press as The Jewish Publication Society would publish it or win a Jewish Book
Council award. Although Rivka and Ben-Zion Dorfman were retired from their
professional lives, this research became their life&#8217;s work and it is a
monumental achievement. <i>Synagogues Without Jews</i> is an excellent addition
to any Jewish library (and would make a lovely gift)--- it is a scholarly, very
readable and beautiful book that is written with compassion and grace. <o:p></o:p></span></p>
