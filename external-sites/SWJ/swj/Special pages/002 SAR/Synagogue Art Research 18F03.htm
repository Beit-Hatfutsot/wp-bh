Synagogue Art Research

 

Toward the close of a long and successful career as an early childhood teacher, Rivka Dorfman activated her latent passion for art and history with extensive studies in art history and Jewish Art at the Hebrew University in Jerusalem. Then she and her husband, Ben-Zion Dorfman, launched a personal project, Synagogue Art Research, to document synagogue art and architecture in Central and Southern Europe. In the 65 weeks of field activity of six summer journeys, they photographed 360 synagogues—in Austria, the Czech Republic, Greece, Hungary, Italy, the Slovak Republic and the former Yugoslavia—especially in cities, towns and villages other than the well-known national capitals. They collected relevant documents and recorded discussions about the former Jewish presence with the rare resident Jews or local officials. At home in Jerusalem, with the help of a devoted corps of volunteer translators, they consolidated the archival and testimonial material and added extensive library research.

ú        In recognition of the achievements of Synagogue Art Research, Rivka was awarded Israel’s "Prize of the Minister of Education and Culture for Innovators in Jewish Culture, 5753-1993."

ú        Their photo-article, "A Fading Heritage: The Fate of Central and Southern European Synagogues," was a featured attraction of the 1983-1992 Decennial Book of the Encyclopaedia Judaica (EJ), 1994. The EJ on CD-ROM contains many more of their photographs.

ú        In commemoration of 50 years after the deportation of Hungarian Jewry, Yad Vashem invited the Dorfmans to create a photo exhibition. Called "From Splendor to Shadow," it was displayed at The Valley of the Communities of Yad Vashem from Oct. 1994 to June 1995. The exhibition was on view again at the World Jewish Congress in Jerusalem during January 1997 and at the Knesset during the Spring that year.

ú        Israel Television’s world news program used some of the Dorfmans’ videotapes and their expertise on synagogues in Bohemia and Moravia for a short documentary in January 1995 to mark the 50th anniversary of the liberation of Auschwitz.

ú        Their 368-page book, Synagogues Without Jews—and the communities that built and used them was published in October 2000 by the Jewish Publication Society. The Jewish Book Council chose it for a National Jewish Book Award 2000. Synagogues Without Jews is undergoing professional translation to Hebrew and is due to be published by Israel’s prestigious publisher, Keter, with updated text revisions and some of the new photos.

ú        From November 2000 through March 2001, the Dorfmans presented their book with 39 lectures on synagogues without Jews at museums, JCCs, university and synagogue audiences on east and west coasts of the USA.

ú        Many buildings that were formerly derelict or storerooms in towns without Jews in Europe have been, or are due to be restored, subject to funding, for cultural use as museums, galleries or concert halls. The Dorfmans’ sixth research trip, of 11 weeks, in 2002, to more than 80 towns of Jewish interest was focused on documenting such synagogue restorations in the Czech and Slovak Republics, Hungary and Austria. The photos of this journey expanded the Dorfman Archive of Synagogue Art to 28,000 images.

ú        This Synagogues Without Jews Website starts from the book and photo archive and, with the help of a new team of volunteers, expands to include new material.
