From this Town

 

A course on Renaissance Art at the Hebrew University, Jerusalem and a lecture by architect David Cassuto on synagogues in Italy motivated a trip to Italy. Our first stop was at the antiquities of Rome’s ancient port, Ostia, where a 4th century synagogue was identified in 1962, at a distance from the main excavated area. It was so undeveloped and neglected that museum officials could not help us find it. But, pointed in the right direction by a passer-by, we went a long way until we discerned the stone columns of the synagogue. The reward lay in seeing the synagogue with its ancient mosaic floor and fallen architrave, engraved with Jewish symbols.

We saw a silent, unattended synagogue in Urbino and well-preserved synagogues in Ferrara, Verona, Parma, Soragna and Mantua. In Sabbioneta, a third-floor synagogue with tall, bronze candelabra is bereft of Jews since the beginning of the 20th century. After we saw these little known synagogues, documenting synagogue art and architecture became a project and a passion. Here was a story that had to be told. Our five seasons of fieldwork took us to 340 synagogues in eight countries. Then followed another seven years of research and writing.

The capitals---Budapest, Bratislava, Prague, Vienna, Rome, Athens, Zagreb, Belgrade---still have small, but viable Jewish communities, and their major synagogues are known and published. It is in the hinterland where they must be documented and perhaps recovered from neglect, adaptation to mundane use, or destruction, before all traces of Jewish presence vanish. From local people, especially among the older ones, there was a readiness to tell us at length about their former Jewish neighbors. One municipal official consoled us when he saw our horror at the shambles of his town’s synagogue, “Aber wir haben einen sh”nen Friedhof.” (But we have a beautiful cemetery.”).

We were excited by the good will and cooperation of many officials and lay people. They helped us make contacts, to enter synagogue buildings and copy documents. They often went out of their way to find people who could share with us memories of life with Jewish neighbors before WWII. Very few put us off or were hostile. One woman, in a tiny, former village synagogue, read our letter of introduction and slammed the door in our faces, “Wir haben das Haus gekauft!” (“We bought this house!”). But many friends are still in contact with us, updating us on restorations and other events.

Since there were few hotels or “zimmer” (pensions) in some of the out-of-the-way places, there were those who invited us to bed and breakfast. We met synagogue caretakers, such as Imre Pogone in Mad, Hungary, where there are no Jews. In Perugia, Italy, all hotels served a cosmeticians’ convention, so the head of the small Jewish community, Sergio Pacifici, observed an old Jewish custom and put us up in the synagogue: an apartment in the old Jewish Quarter.

There were non-Jews who proudly showed us evidence of their family’s Jewish past. The project brought us into contact with the last or almost last Jew in many towns---Holocaust survivors, clinging to vestiges of identity in a rapidly diminishing Jewish reality. Our guide in Hlohovec, Slovakia, was a scholarly, observant Jew, Moshe Gluck, fluent in Hebrew, who lived his last year in Israel. He never rode over the bridge in his town for the approach of which the town had destroyed the community’s synagogue. Whenever he needed to cross it, Moshe walked the whole way over and stopped briefly to recite the Psalms. Other survivors showed us community mementos or told us personal histories.

These villages attract few foreign visitors, so we were often asked the ubiquitous question, “Was your family from this town?” In our wide-ranging and intensive effort on the Jewish built heritage, we felt that we were intrinsic to the local Jewish past. “Yes,” we replied, “our family came from this town.”

Condensed by Dvora Block from Synagogues Without Jews.
