How Fair Are Your Tents, O Jacob

 

The first mention of Jews in St. P”lten is from the 14th century, when St. P”lten Jews loaned money to Vienna citizens Nikolaus Holinus and Johann Hierz. Frenzied riots against the Jews were common, and in 1628, the Jews were ultimately expelled. They were not allowed back into St. P”lten until the nineteenth century. By 1857, a community of 252 Jews in St. P”lten had achieved approval, hired a teacher of religion, established a cemetery, and formally organized a kehillah.

In 1888 the kehillah embarked on the winding path that would eventually lead to construction of a satisfactory synagogue. Attempts to buy property continued until 1911, when an agreement was made to build the new synagogue at the corner of Schul Promenade and Lederergasse.

In the plan, the community called for 220 seats for men and 150 for women at a proposed cost of 95,000 crowns. Kehillot in Austria were prosperous at the time and synagogue architects were busy, but Theodor Schreier tendered a proposal that was adopted. The building committee strove for the best possible workmanship within its budget, employing prominent craftsmen to make the benches, windows, and the bimah. The total construction cost came to 141,240 crowns, about a third higher than the estimate.

After twenty-four years in the preparation – funding, site selection and bureaucratic approval, site purchase, architectural contest, and final plan – the structure went up in 14 months. The dedication was fixed for August 17, 1913, eve of the emperor’s birthday. Patriots all, St. P”lten Jews affixed a plaque in his honor. Prominent dignitaries attended the dedication, and the event had extensive press coverage.

St. P”lten Jews assembled peacefully and prayed in their synagogue for 24 years – as many years as it had taken to create it. Then came the terror of kristalnacht. About 400 people, some in Nazi uniform, descended on the synagogue. They battered down the doors, burst into the sanctuary, smashed the windows, broke the great candelabra, and chopped the furniture. They threw religious books and ritual objects into a bonfire and bellowed Nazi political songs while the books burned.

The synagogue was left to deteriorate after the war. In 1979, however, the Federal Memorials Institute declared the building to be “the most significant Jewish religious structure in lower Austria,” and initiated efforts to save it. The complex was opened in 1984 for new educational and cultural functions as the Institut fur Geschichte der Juden in Osterreich (Institute for the History of Austrian Jewry). The institute’s main goal is to research the history of the Jews in Austria, to emphasize their contribution to Austrian philosophy and culture, and to use this knowledge in the struggle against anti-Semitism.

Martha Keil of the institute sent us an unexpected little treasure in 1995. It is an invitation to the synagogue consecration, addressed to the right honorable Sir Bernhard Osterreicher. The detailed three-page program inspires the following imagined scene on that bright Sunday afternoon:

The gallery ladies in elegant lace and chiffon look down on a sea of men’s cylinder hats below as the assembly rises for the solemn procession and entrance of the Torah scrolls. All chant “Ma Tovu,” “How fair are your tents, O Jacob, your dwellings, O Israel!” (Num. 24:5.) The Rabbi of the Jewish community of Vienna kindles the eternal light for the first time and closes the Holy Ark while the choir chants, “Etz chaim hi” (“For the Torah is a tree of life”; Prov 3:18) The ceremony ends as the congregation and all their distinguished guests rise for the Austrian imperial anthem.

How reassuring it must have been for the loyal Austrian Jews in St. P”lten to have faith in their country on that great festive day. Of about 650 Jews living in St. P”lten before the Second World War, 214 were murdered by the Nazis. A memorial erected in front of the synagogue in 1992 caries their names, engraved in stone.
