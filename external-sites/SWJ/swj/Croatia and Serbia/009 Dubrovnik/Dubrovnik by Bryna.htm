When Our Ships Brought Profit

 

Dubrovnik, also known as Rhacusa or Ragusa, is a port city situated on the Adriatic Coast of Croatia. The Jewish community there goes back as far as 1352, although their presence then was barely tolerated and they were initially denied residence. When Jews did secure residence in Dubrovnik, they became successful traders and helped in the commercial and maritime development of the city. Their success was due in part to the connections they had in Turkey and Italy. By the 16th century, they controlled most of the trade with those regions.

The 16th century also saw an increase in the size of the kehillah, after the expulsions from Spain and Portugal brought many Jews to Dubrovnik. This influx did not sit well with the local Christians and a blood libel was fabricated against the community, one of several that would be thrown at them over the next few centuries. This time the libel resulted in the executions of seven men and the prohibition of trading in port. Eventually the prohibition was overturned, but the Jewish traders were always excessively taxed by the city and they always retained the civic-status of foreigners, never citizens.

Despite the prejudices they faced, the Jews still took pride in their heritage. In 1538, with only 40 people in the community, they organized themselves into a Universitas Haebreorum, with a consul to lead them. In 1750 the problem of who would get one of the four seats of honor near the Holy Ark became such a heated debate that the Dubrovnik authorities had to be called in to settle the dispute. Their solution was to sell each seat for 20 gold pieces--a fee that would then go into the city’s coffers to help them pay for the repair of their cathedral square. That payment would be good for the buyer’s lifetime, and at his death the seat would be sold again.

In 1808, with the French occupying the area, all racial discrimination laws were abolished. This boon to the Jews was marred, however, because of a French sea blockade that interfered with trade. When their new freedoms were revoked again by France’s defeat in 1814, the prominence of Dubrovnik’s port was lost for good. In the 1830s, the Jews shifted their energies from trade to viniculture with substantial success, but they faced another setback in 1880 when disease struck their vineyards.

The rise of Nazism in Europe during the late 1930’s brought Jewish refugees to an impoverished Dubrovnik kehillah. The Italian military took over the city in autumn of 1941, and, despite heavy pressure, resisted transferring the Jews over to German jurisdiction. In June 1943, the Italians shipped the Jews to a concentration camp on the Adriatic island of Rab. When Italy fell that November, many of the island’s internees joined the partisans in the struggle against the Nazis. They were then sent to the battlefront, where they suffered 200 deaths. After the war 23 Jews returned to the city.

In spite of the odds against it, the synagogue in Dubrovnik stands today, largely due to the watchfulness of its congregation. When the Germans entered the city in 1943 one of its members spirited out most of its treasures and left them in the care of Christian friends. In 1991, the synagogue survived the artillery and shelling that erupted during the war between Serbia and Croatia. Now, the community has grown from 23 to 47 members. They have plans for restoring the building and opening a Jewish museum.
