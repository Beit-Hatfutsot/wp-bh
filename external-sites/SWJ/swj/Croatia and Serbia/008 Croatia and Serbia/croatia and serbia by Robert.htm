A Jewish History

 

Within the complex region of Yugoslavia Jews played a minor, yet significant role. Not numerous in any single community, they contributed their varied skills to the development of trade, commerce, and culture. An important dynamic, especially in the twentieth century, was the close contact between the Balkan Sephardic kehillot and their East European Ashkenazic counterparts.

Croatia

Although Jews arrived in Croatia during the Roman era as merchants and traders, the major waves of immigration came after the fifteenth-century expulsions from Spain and Portugal. The Ottoman Sultan, Bayazid II, welcomed the Sephardim whose skills, craftsmanship, and trade connections enhanced his multi-ethnic empire. Communities developed in Split and Dubrovnik, where the Jews enjoyed autonomy in community matters.

The Sephardic newcomers, the native Romaniot Jews and the Ashkenazic settlers together developed a flourishing Jewish life. The Ashkenazim in particular, established thriving settlements in the northern regions under Hungarian rule, in Zagreb and Osijek, now Croatia; and in Novi Sad and Subotica, now Serbia.  By the mid-nineteenth century, Jews were granted full equality by the Croatian parliament.

At the outbreak of World War II, 23,000 Jews lived in Croatia.

Serbia

Jews arrived in the North-Serbian autonomous province of Vojvodina in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, and economic enterprise flourished with the 1782 Tolerance Edict of Joseph II. Newly built towns encouraged Jewish influx to help develop fledgling industries. By the end of the nineteenth century there were nearly 40 Jewish communities in Vojvodina, many of them Orthodox.

A sultan’s charter in 1830 granted Serbia internal independence and conferred on the Jews equality with other citizens. Full rights for Jews were achieved with the Constitution of 1888.

Serbian-Jewish relations reached a high degree of cooperation during World War I, when Jews and Serbs fought side by side against the Central Powers. The post-war years were a Golden Age for the Jews, who enjoyed full equality in the Yugoslav kingdom until the 1938 German annexation of Austria.  The Balkan harmony lasted until the dissolution of the Federal Republic in 1991.

Holocaust and Post-War Period

Aside from the minority Ustasa Nazi collaborators, the general Yugoslav population had no deep identification with the Fascists. Nevertheless, of about 76,000 Jews in Yugoslavia in 1939, only some 16,000 survived. Post-war Croatia counted fewer than 5,000 Jews; Serbia about 4,000; Vojvodina, about 4,000; and some 3,000 in Bosnia. More than half of the Jewish survivors emigrated to Israel, leaving behind 6,000 to 7,000 Jews in 36 shrunken kehillot—down from a pre-war number of 121 communities.

The Jewish community continued to function after World War II by virtue of the overall bonding provided by the Federation of Jewish Communities. The disintegration of Yugoslavia in the 1990s, however, abolished the unifying power of the Federation. The central organization remains functional now only within Serbia.

In Croatia, at the turn of the twenty-first century, only the kehillot of Rijeka, Split, and Dubrovnik maintain their synagogues---with the help of international Jewish organizations, some municipalities, and private donations; the kehillot of Zagreb and Osijek use prayer halls. The only functioning synagogue in Serbia today is in Belgrade. Jews in Novi Sad and in Subotica use prayer halls.

The 1999 Kosovo hostilities put a temporary stop to public funding and restoration activity. The Belgrade kehillah was forced to evacuate many of its women, children and elderly to Budapest for safety, many of whom emigrated permanently. After the war, anti-Semitism became somewhat dormant. Nevertheless, the Jewish future in the former Yugoslavia does not look promising.
