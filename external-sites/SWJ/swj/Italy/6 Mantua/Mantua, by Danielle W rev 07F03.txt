<h1 align=left dir=LTR style='text-align:left'><span style='font-size:28.0pt;
font-family:"Monotype Corsiva";color:#993366'>&#8220;City of Joy&#8221;<o:p></o:p></span></h1>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='text-align:left;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:
embed'><span style='font-size:28.0pt;font-family:"Monotype Corsiva";color:#993366'><![if !supportEmptyParas]>&nbsp;<![endif]><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'>The
Renaissance was a remarkable period for the Jews of Italy. Of all Italian
cities,&nbsp;<i>gloriosia </i>Mantua in the heart of&nbsp;Lombardy led her
sister cities&nbsp;by artistic achievements.&nbsp;The noble house of Gonzaga
and Jewish bankers whom they sponsored&nbsp;provided the necessary support for
a Golden Age in&nbsp;Mantua that Jews knew as&nbsp;<i>Kiryah Alizah,</i> &#8220;City
of Joy.&#8221;</span><span style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <span
lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span
style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>In
the 13<sup>th</sup> and 14<sup>th</sup> centuries, economic pressures motivated
Jewish money lenders from&nbsp;Italy&#8217;s southern regions to migrate northward
to&nbsp;Lombardy. Expulsions from&nbsp;Spain,&nbsp;Sicily, and religious
persecutions in&nbsp;France and&nbsp;Germany also drove Jews to
northern&nbsp;Italy.&nbsp;Mantua attracted many of the ‚migr‚s. The earliest
mention of a cemetery and synagogue in the city, and hence an established&nbsp;<i>kehillah,</i>
is 1420.</span><span style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <span
lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span
style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>The
city began to flourish in 1480, after Marquis Giovanni Francesco II married
Isabella&nbsp;d&#8217;Este. She was one of the foremost Renaissance persons of her
day and set the stage for an era of feminine emancipation that liberated Jewish
women no less than their Christian peers. </span><span lang=EN-GB
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'><o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span
style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Mantua
became the center of a new humanistic spirit in Jewish scholarship. Physician,
scholar, and rabbi Judah Messer Leon published a textbook of logic,&nbsp;<i><u>Nofet
Tzufim</u></i> (Sweetness of Honey), in&nbsp;Mantua before 1480,&nbsp;the first
book by a living writer that was printed in the Hebrew language.</span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <span lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span
style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>The
greatest Jewish historian of the Italian Renaissance was&nbsp;Azariah ben Moses
de Rossi (1510-1578).&nbsp;In <i>Me&#8217;or Einayim</i> (Enlightenment for the
Eyes), he described the disastrous 10-day earthquake of 1571. He noted that
whereas Jews tended to see Divine intervention in the event, other scholars
often searched for natural causes. <i>Me&#8217;or Einayaim </i>aroused opposition
even among the&nbsp;enlightened Jewish scholars in&nbsp;Italy.</span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <span lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span
style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Jewish
physicians in&nbsp;Italy were esteemed for their medical ability. The most
famous family of physicians in&nbsp;Italy was the&nbsp;Portaleone dynasty that
loyally treated Italian nobility in&nbsp;Mantua and&nbsp;Padua for 300 years.</span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <span lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span
style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Dramatizations
of Purim&#8217;s scroll of Esther provided the earliest Jewish theater experience;
they were so popular that Christian neighbors keenly followed the performances
in violation of Church interdiction. As groups expanded their repertoire, they
were invited to perform secular productions at the&nbsp;Gonzaga Court and other
palaces.&nbsp;The first theatrical producer in Italy was the Jewish Judah Leone
de &#8216;Sommi (1525-1590), who also became a prolific playwright. In 16 manuscript
volumes, de&#8217;Sommi wrote pastoral comedies in Italian and some in
Hebrew.&nbsp;De&#8217;Sommi &#8216;s pastoral drama for a Purim performance,&nbsp;<i>Zakut
B&#8217;dihutah d&#8217;Kidushin</i> (The Comedy of the Wedlock), rediscovered and
published in the 1940s, is the first original drama written in Hebrew.</span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <span lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:Verdana'><span
style='mso-tab-count:1'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Mantua
led the new theater arts of&nbsp;Italy, while&nbsp;the <i>kehillah </i>provided
dramatists, impresarios, performers, musicians and often the dancers for the
many spectacles. The greatest Jewish musician of the period was&nbsp;Salomone
de&#8217; Rossi. Partly influenced by&nbsp;Palestrina and an occasional collaborator
of&nbsp; Monteverdi,&nbsp;he published compositions from 1589 to 1623,
introducing the musical spirit of the Renaissance. His works were composed of
passages from the liturgy, including psalms, hymns and prayers for Sabbath and
holidays. His cantata&nbsp;<i>Hashirim Asher l&#8217;Shlomo</i> (The Songs of
Solomon), a collection of 33 songs he wrote for the synagogue, was recently
republished in a modern edition in&nbsp;New York.</span><span style='font-family:
Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <span lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
text-indent:19.85pt;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:
Verdana'>German mercenaries hired by Austrian Emperor Ferdinand II set siege to
Mantua in 1626 and the city felt on the 9<sup>th</sup> of Av, in 5390 (1630).
After it was&nbsp;sacked, Jews were expelled on 3 days notice. During the
following four months, wagon convoys rolled out of&nbsp;Mantua carrying booty
from ghetto, palaces and town. Later that year, after obtaining the emperor&#8217;s
permission, only about a thousand of the 1600 survivors chose to return to
their looted homes in&nbsp;Mantua. The reorganized&nbsp; <i>kehillah </i>was a
shadow of its former self and never regained its past splendor.</span><span
style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'> <span lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal dir=LTR style='margin-bottom:12.0pt;text-align:left;
text-indent:19.85pt;direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span style='font-family:
Verdana'>Ever shrinking, the&nbsp;<i>kehillah </i>numbered 669 in 1931 and by
1980 the 160 Jews who still lived in&nbsp;Mantua maintained only a solitary
extant synagogue. It was the last of six synagogues they had used in the
nineteenth&nbsp;century when they numbered more than 2,000. The building has
been declared a national architectural monument. One of the <i>kehillah&#8217;s </i>real
treasures was a precious archive, now kept far away in the Vatican Library
(Codex Rossi 555).</span><span style='font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:
EN-GB'> <span lang=EN-GB><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>

direction:ltr;unicode-bidi:embed'><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:8.0pt;
font-family:Verdana;mso-ansi-language:EN-GB'>Condensed by Danielle Wegman from <i>Synagogues
Without Jews.<o:p></o:p></i></span></p>
