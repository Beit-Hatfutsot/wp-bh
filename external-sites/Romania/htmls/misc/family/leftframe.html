ike everywhere in the Diaspora, family life was important for the Jews of Romania. Strong family ties helped them to overcome difficult situations, to start successful economic ventures and improve their social status. Family solidarity proved to be essential during hard times, particularly at the time of the Holocaust, when it helped many of the deportees to survive the ghettoes and concentration camps in Transnistria. After WW2 it was chiefly the family that provided the principal framework and tools for the continuous attachment to Jewish values and Zionist ideals. Moreover, Jews who emigrated to other countries have continued to take care of their relatives left behind as well as of their ancestors' graves.

The wedding of Aurica Emanuel and Mircea Fenster took place in Ploesti, Wallachia, on November 28, 1926. The bride's father, David Emanuel, owned one of the first movie theaters in Romania. The wedding party was celebrated in the theater's garden, close to the family home located at the upper story of the theater's building. The photographer, who was the bride's brother and studied cinematography at Paramount studios, died in Bucharest in 1943; the groom passed away in Israel in 1994 aged 92, while the bride lives in Israel and the toddler at the end of the video clip is now a resident of New Jersey, USA.
