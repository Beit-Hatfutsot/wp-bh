he religious life of the Romanian Jews was influenced by the spreading of the Hasidism, which was strong especially in Bukovina, Bessarabia, Moldovia, and Northern Transylvania. In other regions, where the impact of emancipation was more powerful, the majority preferred the modern mainly neologist and reformed Judaism. Sometimes tensions mounted between followers of different currents, best illustrated by the conflict within Bucharest community that led to the expulsion from Romania of Rabbi Meir Leibush ben Yechiel Michael (1809-1879), a strong opponent of modern Judaism. A special mention should be made of the Landau, Taubes, and Friedman rabbinical families whose distinguished members guided the spiritual life of many generations of Romanian Jews. In 1921 the office of Chief Rabbi of the Romanian Jews as representative of the "Mosaic Cult" was established and Rabbi Dr. Jacob Niemirower (1872 - 1939) was the first to be elected to this position.
