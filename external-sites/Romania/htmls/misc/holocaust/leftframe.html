The vast majority of the Jewish population of Bessarabia and Bukovina was exterminated by German troops with the active help of the Romanian authorities and military, especially the Jandarmerie. Massacres took place in numerous places soon after the area was captured from the Soviet Union in 1941; the remaining Jews were later deported to ghettos and concentration camps in Transnistria where many died of hunger, disease, hard work, and executions. The Jews of Northern Transylvania were under Hungarian rule from 1940 to 1944 and their fate was similar to that of the Hungarian Jewry: during the spring and summer of 1944, they were deported to Auschwitz death camp where more than 90% were exterminated.

The Jewish population of Moldavia, Wallachia, and Southern Transylvania remained under direct Romanian rule for the entire period of the Holocaust. Jews' rights were restricted, and they were compelled to forced labor, special taxation and war contributions, expelled from schools, working places, and homes, deprived of a decent living, many thousands were imprisoned and deported to Transnistria, and tens of thousands were victims of pogroms, death sentences and summary executions.

During the Holocaust period the Union of the Jewish Communities, under the leadership of Chief Rabbi Dr. Alexander Yehuda Safran, strove to prevent further worsening of the situation and succeeded, with the help of a number of righteous gentiles, in canceling some anti-Jewish measures, notably a decision to deport all Romanian Jews to concentration camps in Poland in July 1942.
