lready by the middle of the 16th century a few synagogues functioned in various Romanian towns, like Piatra Neamt. Their number reached its apogee during the 1930's when in Jassy alone, for example, there existed 112 synagogues. In the regions with strong mainly hasidic Eastern European traditions, the simpler prayer houses (shtibel, shil) often of wooden structure and ornamented with traditional Jewish folk art motifs were predominant. Starting with the middle of the 19th century, new impressive synagogues reflecting the improved social and economic status of the communities were built in the eclectic Moorish-Romanesque-Gothic style characteristic of Central Europe, especially in Bucharest and in Transylvanian cities.
