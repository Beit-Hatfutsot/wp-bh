t was only during the first decades of the 20th century that the Jewish communities were recognized as public bodies. In Transylvanian towns generally there existed separate neologist, orthodox, and status-quo communities which had different traditions and internal structures from those of the other regions. The communities sustained traditional Jewish institutions (synagogues, ritual baths, hevrot-kadisha etc.) with a special care for Jewish education. After WW2 all Jewish communities were united into The Federation of the Jewish Communities of Romania. In 1960 there still existed 153 organized Jewish communities with 841 synagogues and 67 ritual baths, but by 1975 their number diminished to 70 communities with 150 synagogues. The main task of the Jewish communities during the last decades was to provide assistance to the aging small Jewish population and to assure Jewish education and cultural life for the very few members of the young generations.
