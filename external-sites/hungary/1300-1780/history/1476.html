Painting by Béla Vízkelety (1825–1864), 1864

Around 1477 King Matthias I (Corvinus) established the institution of the “Jewish Prefecture” (Prefectus Judaeorum). The prefect was a Jew, and he was the secular leader of the community who had a title in the court as a result of his position. The office of the Jewish principal was held by the Mendel family of Buda, who were also known as “Jewish Princes” (Princeps Judaeorum).

According to contemporary descriptions, the wedding celebrations of King Matthias and Queen Beatrix were attended by a delegation of some one hundred splendidly dressed Jews carrying Torah scrolls and a flag, who came to present their gifts to the royal couple. They covered their heads with tallits, recited prayers and sang hymns. All the costs of one day of the royal wedding ceremonies – which lasted several weeks – were financed by the Jews of Buda.
